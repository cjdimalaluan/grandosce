Master Equipment Checklist

Higher Cortical & Mental Status

Sequence Rule: The Mental Status Exam (MSE) MUST be performed first among all neurological exams. It ensures the exam is valid; if the patient cannot comprehend instructions, subsequent motor/sensory testing is invalid.
Sensorium Pearl: A decreased sensorium CANNOT be produced by a single, one-sided cerebral hemisphere lesion (unless it is massively edematous and space-occupying). Normal sensorium strictly requires: 1. Bilateral Reticular Formation, 2. Bilateral Thalami, 3. Bilateral Hemispheres.

Station 2 Procedural Script

Somatosensory & Meningeal

Proprioception Handling: When testing joint position sense, you must hold the distal phalanx strictly on the SIDES (medial/lateral). Pressing the nail bed (top/bottom) stimulates pressure receptors and invalidates the proprioceptive test.
Malingering Check: To test for malingering/psychogenic deficits, occasionally ask the patient for sensation even if there is NO stimulus applied. True deficits follow sharp dermatomal demarcations.
Meningeal Contraindication: Do NOT perform nuchal rigidity testing if the patient has suspected cervical instability (e.g., severe Trauma, severe Rheumatoid Arthritis).

Station 4 Procedural Script

Theoretical & Faculty Guide Questions

These are the exact required Q&A pairings derived from the official Faculty Station Guide. Click to reveal.

Combo 1: Pathways
Trace the OLFACTORY pathway sequentially.
1. Olfactory receptor cells (1st order) →
2. Olfactory nerves (pass through cribriform plate) →
3. Olfactory bulb (synapse with mitral cells at glomeruli) →
4. Olfactory tract (axons of mitral cells) →
5. Olfactory striae (Lateral to primary cortex, Medial across anterior commissure) →
6. Olfactory cortex (temporal lobe).
Combo 1: Pathways
What two nerves are involved in testing olfaction, and why avoid irritating odors?
CN I (Olfactory): For true olfaction.
CN V (Trigeminal): Senses temperature and irritating substances (e.g., ammonia, alcohol).
Irritating odors stimulate CN V, giving a false positive result in a patient who is actually anosmic.
Combo 1: Pathways
Trace the OPTIC pathway sequentially.
1. Retina ganglionic layer (optic nerve fibers) →
2. Optic disc → Optic canal →
3. Optic Chiasm (nasal fibers decussate, temporal stay ipsilateral) →
4. Optic Tract →
5. Lateral Geniculate Body (posterior thalamus) →
6. Optic Radiations (upper/parietal = inferior visual field; lower/temporal = superior field) →
7. Visual Cortex (calcarine sulcus, occipital lobe).
Combo 3: Meningeal
What causes meningeal irritation?
Irritation of the subarachnoid space. Common causes include:
1. Infectious meningitis (bacterial/viral)
2. Subarachnoid hemorrhage (blood is highly irritating)
3. Brain and meningeal neoplasms
4. Stroke affecting an area adjacent to CSF spaces.
Combo 3: Meningeal
If there is meningeal irritation, what signs and resistance do you expect?
(+) Nuchal rigidity: Neck flexion limitation or pain upon flexion.
(+) Brudzinski's: Involuntary flexion of hips & knees upon neck flexion.
(+) Kernig's: Pain in the hamstrings/lower back and resistance to further extension (patient cannot extend knee beyond ~135 degrees).
Combo 3: Meningeal
If an unconscious patient has a rigid neck in ALL directions AND all 4 limbs are rigid, is this a meninges problem? Yes or No?
NO. Neck flexion is specifically used to test for nuchal rigidity (meningeal irritation). Rigidity of the neck in all directions combined with 4-limb rigidity suggests diffuse severe pathology (e.g., decerebrate/decorticate posturing or massive brainstem involvement), not a simple meningeal sign.
Faculty Guide: Motor
Trace the afferent-efferent arc of muscle stretch reflexes.
Muscle spindle stretch receptor (Afferent sensory fiber) → Dorsal root ganglion → Dorsal horn → Synapse directly on Alpha motor neuron in Anterior horn (Integration) → Ventral root (Efferent motor fiber) → Neuromuscular junction → Muscle contraction. (This is a monosynaptic arc).
Faculty Guide: Motor
Trace the corticospinal tract.
Primary Motor Cortex (Precentral Gyrus) → Corona Radiata → Posterior Limb of Internal Capsule → Cerebral Peduncles (Midbrain) → Pons → Medullary Pyramids (Decussation of lateral tract) → Lateral Corticospinal Tract in Spinal Cord → Anterior Horn (synapse with LMN).
Faculty Guide: Motor
Enumerate methods of eliciting extensor plantar responses aside from Babinski.
Used when the soles are sensitive or damaged:
Chaddock: Stroking lateral malleolus.
Oppenheim: Sliding knuckles down tibial crest.
Gordon: Squeezing the calf muscle.
Faculty Guide: Motor
Explain how to document reflex findings using the standard reflex stick figure.
Draw a stick figure representing the patient facing you. Document the 0-4+ grade at the respective joints: Biceps/Brachioradialis at elbows, Triceps above elbows, Patellar at knees, Achilles at ankles. Append an up arrow (↑) or down arrow (↓) at the feet for the plantar response. Include superficial reflexes (jaw jerk, abdominal) if tested.
Faculty Guide: Stn 1
Identify the cortical regions involved in aphasias, apraxias, and agnosias.
Aphasias: Broca's (Inferior Frontal Gyrus), Wernicke's (Superior Temporal Gyrus).
Apraxias: Premotor cortex, Supplementary motor area, or Parietal lobe.
Agnosias: Occipitotemporal (fusiform) or Parietal regions.
Faculty Guide: Stn 2
Explain the swinging flashlight test and RAPD.
Detects a Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD). When light swings from the healthy eye to the affected eye, both pupils paradoxically dilate. This occurs because the sick optic nerve generates a weaker afferent signal than the healthy eye did, leading to less efferent constriction.
Faculty Guide: Stn 3
Distinguish sensory ataxia from cerebellar ataxia.
Sensory Ataxia: Caused by DCML lesions. Worsens significantly when eyes are closed (Positive Romberg) because visual compensation is removed.
Cerebellar Ataxia: Caused by defective cerebellar integration. The patient is uncoordinated and unsteady regardless of whether eyes are open or closed (Romberg is inapplicable).
